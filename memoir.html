Part One

The Woman Who Needed Energy

Writing my mother's story has taken me to places I sometimes wish I could avoid. Parts of her history are heavy enough that it would be easier to close the door and move on.

Growing up, she was your typical Asian tiger mom — perpetually yelling at me and adept at finding a way to make whatever I was doing wrong.

But strangely, the deeper I've gone into writing her story, the closer I feel to humanity. I understand why she raised me the way she did, the generations of pain that she's fought and sliced through. And slowly, I'm beginning to learn that the pain doesn't have to cripple us. Systems get stronger from stress.

Family pain moves quietly across generations, like fire passing through a forest, until someone finally turns around and faces the flames.

I'm documenting my mother's life so I can understand her and quiet the flames from passing through.

In conversation

"Mom, you have a lot of energy."

"Adrienne, I needed to have energy. I had no other choice. When I was raising you, I'd get up at 5am every morning and work until 12 midnight. I drive my car to Stonestown, and then taken the MUNI to downtown, where I worked at Jacobson's lab. When I came home, I'd cook dinner and the family would eat. After dinner, I'd work on our family lab business in the garage, painting porcelain on teeth until midnight. When I left the garage, everyone in the house was already asleep and I'd go to bed, and begin again five hours later."

She didn't have energy. She needed it.

Documenting her story is not only about honoring the past, it's a reminder of the generations of shoulders I stand on. Because of her, I was able to start life on third base. And because of that, I don't want to let a single thing I have go to waste.

outside Saigon · the youngest of ten
Part Two

The Youngest of Ten

My mother was the youngest of ten children, raised in a two-bedroom house outside Saigon, before it was renamed Ho Chi Minh City, before the war rewrote everyone's future. Her family earned just enough to survive, never enough to rest.

In her world, boys mattered more. Sons carried the family name; daughters carried the work.

Her oldest brother, Bubba, was treated like royalty. She cooked for him, washed his clothes, ironed his shirts. If the creases weren't straight, he yelled. That was normal then. No one called it unfair. It was simply the order of things.

Girls stayed home.

My mother rarely left the house. Only for haircuts, and even then she had to return immediately. So when she describes herself as brazen, she means something small but enormous: wanting something enough to risk disobedience.

She once became obsessed with bubblegum wrappers printed with tiny Japanese dolls, each one dressed differently, each face slightly new. No one sold them in her town. She wanted them so badly she did something unthinkable.

One day, still a child, she snuck out alone and took a bus downtown to buy them. Her father never found out.

She still remembers the thrill more than the gum.

Part Three

Running Until Somewhere Held

Her parents themselves were refugees long before she was born. They had fled China when Japan invaded and men were being forced into military service. A friend helped them escape in the middle of the night. From China to Hong Kong, then Cambodia, and finally Vietnam, moving wherever survival allowed.

In Cambodia they survived doing housework for distant relatives. Life was hard everywhere. Eventually a cousin in Vietnam offered something different: a trade. Dentistry. Making teeth by hand. They barely spoke Vietnamese, but skill mattered more than language.

That trade would later become the family's lifeline.

When her older siblings left home, my mother, still young, had to quit school to learn dentistry herself. Childhood ended quietly, replaced by responsibility.

the South China Sea · 1975
Part Four

The Escape

By the 1970s, Vietnam was splitting apart. When the Americans withdrew in 1975, the North took control of the South. Families like hers feared what came next: property seized, money confiscated, neighbors watching neighbors.

Freedom became something you could lose overnight.

So they paid smugglers who knew a way out. My mother carried the grandson king Go Som with her as they left for the ocean.

Part Five

When Marriage Arrives at Your Door

Ann, or Yin, as her family called her, was the youngest daughter and the last to marry. In her world, marriage was not romance. It was logistics.

Matches arrived through relatives, whispers, reputation. One afternoon, her sister told her, almost casually:

"There's a man at the door. He wants to see you. Handsome… but older."

The man was in his forties. He studied her carefully before speaking.

"I'm thinking of marrying you to my son. Would you like to meet him?"

She said yes because saying no was not really an option. A few days later he returned with his son, Quang. They exchanged polite sentences that barely filled the room.

"How are you?"

"I'm… good."

He had a stutter. She didn't notice, or maybe she chose not to. After they left, her mother asked, "What do you think?"

"He seems okay."

"Then you will get engaged."

And that was that.

Her mother Po was a gentle woman shaped by survival — fleeing war in China, rebuilding life again in Vietnam, raising eight children through scarcity. She believed manners protected daughters. A well-raised girl could secure stability. Stability was love.

Unmarried young people were being sent to rural labor farms, endless days planting potatoes under supervision, far from family. Women often returned pregnant. Everyone understood why without speaking about it.

Yin was nineteen. Unmarried. Afraid.

"Maybe marriage is safer," her mother told her. Yin agreed.

Warnings came too late. Friends whispered that the Tran family was harsh. Someone claimed Quang was unstable. Po traveled to their village herself, asking directly if the rumors were true.

"No," his sister assured her. "He's perfectly fine."

Po believed people because she herself was honest. So the wedding went forward.

Part Six

Inside her husband's house

After the marriage, Yin left home and moved into her husband's family house. Overnight, she became daughter-in-law, servant, outsider.

She cooked. Cleaned. Washed clothes by hand. The family owned a dental clinic. Every afternoon at two, the household rested. Everyone slept, except Yin. She cooked through the heat while others napped.

One day her mother-in-law dropped a live fish in front of her.

"Make canh chua."

Yin had never killed an animal before. At nineteen, she stood frozen, trying to remember how her own mother cooked. Finally she picked up a hammer and struck the fish again and again until it stopped moving.

When she served dinner, her mother-in-law pushed the bowl across the table.

"You think this is cooked?"

Sometimes freshly washed clothes were thrown back into dirty water because they were "wrong." Nothing she did passed inspection.

Pain, passed forward like inheritance.

A year later came her father's death anniversary, a sacred day meant to be spent with family. She asked permission to go home. "No."

Her nephew traveled hours to retrieve her, pleading on her behalf. "No," her mother-in-law repeated. "When I married, I wasn't allowed to go home either."

Her nephew whispered a plan: "Take your jewelry. We'll leave. Don't come back."

Yin gathered everything she owned, held her one-year-old daughter Mai, and ran. Behind her, footsteps. Her mother-in-law caught up, grabbing at the child.

"Leave if you want, but the baby stays. She belongs to us."

They pulled in opposite directions. "You'll have more children!" her nephew shouted. "Let her go!"

Yin tightened her arms.

"No. She is my daughter."

Her nephew eventually left without her. Yin returned to the house she had tried to escape. She cried for days and stopped eating.

"If you don't want food," her mother-in-law said, "then don't eat."

Even rice became a test. There was an electric rice cooker locked safely behind glass, but Yin was forbidden to use it. Instead she cooked over charcoal, crouched beside the fire, adjusting heat grain by grain, watching carefully so nothing burned.

Perfection was demanded. Comfort was withheld.

And still, she endured.

Lisbon · where the shark music fades
Part Seven

The Life She Made Possible

Learning about her childhood helps me understand why my mom is the way she is. Why she screams at me when I accidentally spill the coffee cup she's holding. To me, it's an accident but to her, it's a catastrophe.

Growing up, small moments rarely stayed small. A parking spot too tight, a driver moving too slowly could detonate the entire afternoon. As a child, home felt like living with background danger, as if something terrible might happen at any moment, even when everything was fine. I learned to listen for changes in tone, facial expressions, the way a sentence landed.

Only recently has the shark music in my head started to fade.

Moving to Lisbon was part of that. Every day I wake up feeling so lucky, thinking, wow — I am living inside things ten-year-old me would have dreamed of.

But the shark music didn't quiet because of the big, cinematic moments. It faded because of lots of small moments.

It's the man at the café recognizing me before I speak. "Cherry matcha with oat milk. Less ice." That small feeling of being remembered without asking for it.

The way my tennis instructor Paulo tells me, "Nosotros ficamos feliz quando podemos transmitir o conhecimento" — We feel happy when we can pass knowledge on.

It's the way when I'm riding in an Uber and I yawn, the driver gently asks, "Estas cansada, triste?" The question lands strangely, because at home feelings were something pushed down, to be carried quietly, and not take up space. But he notices, and wants me to tell him more.

It's the way my Israeli friend Yair calls me his sister, and when I tell him I'm excited for his wedding, he tells me, "I'm excited more. My sister will meet my mom and my other sister." Closeness doesn't need to be earned — you're already inside.

Nothing dramatic happens in these moments. And somewhere along the way,
without announcing itself, the shark music stops playing.

I spill something and don't immediately brace for anger. Moments stop feeling like the pause before an explosion.

I learn that mistakes are survivable. That love can sound calm.

The life I live now — joyful, spacious, full of possibility — is built from thousands of invisible decisions my mom had to endure.
